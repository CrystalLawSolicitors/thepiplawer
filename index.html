Personal Independence Payment (PIP)

What is PIP and who is it for?

Personal Independence Payment (PIP) is a UK benefit that helps with the extra costs of living with a long-term health condition or disability. It is not means-tested and can be paid whether or not you work.

PIP has two components:

  • Daily living ‚Äì support with everyday activities.
  • Mobility ‚Äì support with getting around.

Decisions are based on how your condition affects you against set activities and descriptors, not just your diagnosis. TPL helps you understand that framework and explain your difficulties clearly.

Key facts (overview)
  • PIP is assessed by the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP).
  • You can receive PIP as well as other benefits and earnings.
  • You can challenge decisions and appeal if you disagree.

This website is independent guidance, not an official government site. For official information and current rates, always check GOV.UK.

Steps & criteria

The PIP journey, from first claim to appeal

1. Check basic eligibility

  • Age requirements for new claims (usually 16+ and under State Pension age).
  • Long-term condition or disability (expected to last at least 12 months).
  • Real-world difficulties with daily living and/or mobility.

We help you link your day-to-day difficulties to the PIP activities and descriptors used in the assessment.

2. Start a PIP claim

  • Initial telephone call or online start (where available).
  • PIP2 form ‚Äì ‚ÄúHow your disability affects you‚Äù.
  • Gathering medical evidence and supporting statements.

Our guidance helps you avoid common mistakes and explain things clearly without downplaying your difficulties.

3. Assessment & decision

  • Assessment with a health professional (phone, video or face-to-face).
  • DWP decision letter with your scores and award details.

We explain how to read the decision and what your options are if you disagree with it.

4. Mandatory reconsideration

If you think the decision is wrong, the first step is usually a mandatory reconsideration.

  • Explain why you believe the decision is wrong.
  • Point out where the report or decision missed key facts.
  • Include any new or clearer evidence.

TPL can help structure your arguments and letters in a clear, decision-maker-friendly way.
