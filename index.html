Personal Independence Payment (PIP)

What is PIP and who is it for?

Personal Independence Payment (PIP) is a UK benefit to help with the extra costs of living with a long-term health condition or disability. It is not means-tested and can be paid whether or not you work.

PIP has two components:

  • Daily living ‚Äì help with everyday activities.
  • Mobility ‚Äì help with getting around.

Decisions are based on how your condition affects you in relation to specific ‚Äúactivities‚Äù and ‚Äúdescriptors‚Äù. TPL helps you understand that framework and express your day-to-day difficulties clearly.

Key facts
  • PIP claims and decisions are handled by the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP).
  • You can receive PIP as well as other benefits and earnings.
  • You can challenge decisions and appeal if you disagree.

TPL is an independent guidance website, not an official government site. For formal rules and rates, always check the latest information on GOV.UK.

Steps & criteria

The PIP journey ‚Äì from first claim to appeal

1. Check basic eligibility

  • Age requirements for new claims (usually 16+ and under State Pension age).
  • A condition or disability expected to last at least 12 months.
  • Real-world difficulties with daily living and/or mobility.

We help you link your difficulties to the descriptors used in the PIP assessment, rather than focusing only on diagnosis labels.

2. Start a PIP claim

  • Initial phone call or online start (where available).
  • PIP2 form ‚Äì ‚ÄúHow your disability affects you‚Äù.
  • Collecting medical letters, reports and supporting statements.

Our guidance explains common pitfalls when filling in forms and how to describe your needs honestly without minimising them.

3. Assessment & decision

  • Assessment with a health professional (phone, video or in person).
  • DWP decision letter setting out points and award level.

We help you understand what the assessor looks at, how to read the decision letter and what to consider doing next.

4. Mandatory reconsideration

If you believe the decision is wrong, the first challenge is usually a mandatory reconsideration.

  • Explain clearly why the decision is incorrect.
  • Highlight where facts were missed or misunderstood.
  • Add new or clearer evidence where possible.

TPL provides structure and example wording you can adapt for your own reconsideration request.

5. Tribunal appeal

If the reconsideration does not resolve matters, you can appeal to an independent tribunal.

  • Appeal form or online appeal process.
  • Short written statement of your main points.
  • Preparing for questions the tribunal may ask.

In suitable cases, TPL can signpost you to advocacy or solicitor representation, including via Crystal Law Solicitors.

6. Reviews & changes

PIP awards are usually time-limited and subject to review. We can help you prepare for:

  • Planned award reviews and ‚Äúlight-touch‚Äù reviews.
  • Changes of circumstances that you need to report.
  • What to consider if your condition improves or worsens.
